Capital Infusion

A practice known as CAPITAL INFUSION involves an investor with a financial stake in the company injecting money into struggling or starting businesses. Another definition of capital infusion is the process of transferring funds from a profitable unit, division, or subsidiary of a business to a struggling unit in an effort to give the struggling unit new life. This kind of capital infusion is appropriate for large corporations whose operations are divided across numerous subsidiaries, each of which has an interest in the business and which strives to be profitable overall.

One type of Capital Infusion is where a company having multiple units & subsidiaries take surplus funds from one unit and injects it in another unit. For example, if unit A is making profits and unit B is barely making any returns, the company will take the surplus profit from unit A and inject in unit B with the motive to give it required boost. The funds are circulated within the organization and this practice is termed as internal capital infusion.

Another type of capital infusion is where funds are taken from outside the firm. Some startup businesses may have a solid idea or potential, but they lack the necessary resources to fully take off. A venture capitalist may decide to intervene and offer the financial boost required to make the required upgrades, including buying machinery, renting a suitable location, paying personnel, and outfitting office space. Professionals known as venture capitalists give beginning businesses the much-needed funding infusion. The investor may also be a generous donor or a corporate partner having a financial interest in the success of the venture. A person of this type will only invest with the goal of recovering their capital and turning a profit after the company is fully functioning and generating a profit.
